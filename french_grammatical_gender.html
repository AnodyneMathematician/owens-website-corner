An Analysis of French Grammatical Gender

Backstory/Goal:
Some sources and people I have met like to describe grammatical gender as “random” or “unpredictable.” While it is true that in certain languages it is hard to tease out patterns and rules for grammatical gender, I want to show that in French it isn’t so bad, and that with just a few rules we can correctly predict the grammatical gender of a significant majority of nouns.

However, when I looked at reference grammars, research papers, and other resources, the vast majority presented French grammatical gender rules in a confusing and haphazard way. These explanations often felt more like trivia than something systematic, or like linguistic babble that an ordinary learner would struggle to understand, let alone remember.

As such, my main goal is to provide a small set of rules that are easy to remember and effective in determining French grammatical gender, so that a student with little background in linguistics can have a much less painful experience learning it.

To do this, I conducted my own data analysis of French nouns.

Methods (and critiques):
For most of this project, I used a quick and somewhat informal method to obtain results. I found a list (1) of approximately 2000 supposedly frequent French nouns and used this as my initial dataset.

I then reviewed the entries to get a sense of the data quality. During this stage, I also removed a small subset of plural entries.

Finally, to validate my findings, I used the Python package pylexique, based on the larger Le Lexique (4) database, and computed accuracy on small batches (fewer than 30 items). This allowed me to check whether the ~87% accuracy I observed was realistic, and whether I had made any major errors.

Because of this, I want to note the following regarding the data:

(A) The data was only of moderate quality. The French dataset was much better than those provided for other languages on the same website; however, I was unable to confirm how “frequent” words were selected. Nonetheless, a quick inspection suggested that the words were sufficiently common, which was adequate for my goal of maximising accuracy in everyday conversation rather than in highly technical or archaic contexts. The data source appears, based on my observation, to consist of common English nouns that were translated into French. In some cases, multiple English nouns mapped to the same French word (e.g. beat, rhythm → le rythme).

(B) I made many decisions regarding data selection and validation in order to complete the project quickly. Ideally, I would prefer a more rigorous and airtight approach to accuracy. I may revisit this in the future if there is interest or if I have more time.

(C) Due to concerns about data validity, the true accuracy for most real-world use cases is likely slightly lower than reported. However, my validation suggests that it is not drastically lower than the stated figures.

Exploration of data:
One of the most useful resources for me, when exploring the data was this blog post by Alexis Huet (2).

With a few additions and modifications, here is my reproduction of a bar chart showing the final letter of French nouns and the frequency of each gender (blue bars on the left indicate masculine nouns, and red bars on the right indicate feminine nouns):

We can notice several interesting patterns from this plot. However, one particularly notable observation is that nearly all feminine nouns are concentrated among words that end in -e and -n.

This leads us to the following rule:

The Base/Simple Rule (77% accuracy):
Ends in -e or -n → Feminine (F)
Ends in any other letter → Masculine (M)

Amazingly, if you learned only this rule to predict gender in French, you would be correct nearly four-fifths of the time!

Especially after looking at grammatical gender in other languages - this is somewhat mind-boggling tol me :). Thankfully, this means that for the rest of our analysis, we only need to gain a few more percentage points in order to develop a system that is highly accurate at determining French grammatical gender.

Looking more closely at the bar chart, I wondered whether there might be a way to isolate or separate masculine nouns from feminine ones among words ending in -e, -n, or -r. If so, there would be very little uncertainty remaining, and our task would essentially be complete.

So, I created bar charts that only included words ending in -n, -e, or -r, and then examined the masculine/feminine breakdown based on the second-to-last letter.

From the first of these bar charts, I developed the following rule for nouns ending in -n:

-on/-n rule (+4% → 81%):
Does the noun end in -on?
No → Masculine
Yes →
-Is it long (≥6 letters)? → Feminine
-Is it short? → Probably masculine

Among words ending in -e, -n, and -r, those ending in -n show by far the most regular pattern. As such, we are able to improve our accuracy for -n words from 60% to 89%.

The -r rule and comments on other non-e words (+ <1%)

Because such a large proportion of -r words are masculine, we are only able to improve prediction slightly. As a result, this rule is more useful for identifying endings that are reliably masculine rather than for strong predictive power.

For nouns ending in -r:
-r rule (+ <1%):
Does it end in -eur?
No → Masculine
Yes →
-Is it long (≥6 letters)? → Probably masculine
-Is it short? → Possibly feminine

(Prediction heuristic: short + ends in -eur → guess feminine; otherwise masculine)

Notes on -o,-a,-x:
Words ending in -o:
Check whether the word is a nickname or abbreviation, and then infer the gender from the full form (e.g. moto → motocyclette).
(Common exception: la vidéo)

-a words: Although more data is needed, feminine words here seem to include some holdovers from Latin (e.g. la villa) and loanwords for food or drink (e.g. la vodka, la pizza).

-x words: Feminine words ending in -x often end in -ix, but they are still in the minority. As such, it is generally easier to guess masculine for words ending in -x.

Before moving on to the main -e rules, it is worth mentioning a regular pattern for words ending in -é:

-é rule (+2% → 83%):
Ends in -té?
Yes → Feminine (F)
No → Masculine (M)

e rules:
Applying the same approach as before, when we examine the second-to-last letter of words ending in -e, we find that there is no single clean pattern.

Nonetheless, simply guessing feminine already gives about 70% accuracy for -e words. If we can increase this to 80% or higher, we can build a highly effective system for predicting the gender of almost any French noun.

When analysing the second-to-last letter, we observe a range of two-letter endings:

Common -e endings and percentage feminine:

-se (97%)
-ie (93%)
-ce (84%)
-te (79%)
-ne (71%)
-re (59%)
-le (58%)
-me (47%)
-ge (25%)

(Note: for other endings such as -be or -pe, nouns are also often likely to be feminine.)

From this, we can identify endings that are strongly associated with feminine gender:
-se, -ce, -ie, -te

A mnemonic I use for remmembering what second letter ending's of e-words tend to be feminine is: SCITECH.

However, endings such as -ge, -le, -me, -ne, -re are more complex. To refine predictions, we can examine the third and fourth letters.

Refined rules:

-ge rule (+2%) (83% → 85%):
-age → Masculine
-rge → Feminine
Other → Masculine

(Assume masculine unless -rge)
Notable exceptions: la plage, l’image

-re rule (+1%) (85% → 86%):
-ure → Feminine
-ère → Probably feminine
-tre → Likely masculine
If -ire:
-oire → Likely masculine
Otherwise → Likely feminine

Others -re endings → ~55% feminine
(Assume feminine unless -tre or -oire)

Note: If you see a doubled consonant before -e (e.g. -tte, -lle), there is about a 90% chance the word is feminine.

-le rule (+1%) (86% → 87%):
-lle → Feminine
-ule → Feminine
-ile → Possibly feminine
Other → Likely masculine

(If -lle, -ule, or -ile → feminine; otherwise masculine)

Minor e rules:
-me rule (+ <1%):
Short (<6 letters) → Probably feminine
Long (>6 letters) → Likely masculine
6 letters → roughly equal; default to feminine

-de rule (+ <1%):
-ide → Masculine
Otherwise → Feminine

-ue rule (+ <1%):
Not -que → Feminine (≈95%)
If -que:
-ique → Likely feminine
Otherwise → Possibly masculine
(Assume feminine unless -que but not -ique)

If you’ve managed to learn all that, congratulations!
You should now be able to predict the gender of French nouns with around 85% accuracy (and 88% within this dataset).

I could continue adding more rules, but I don’t believe my current dataset has sufficient resolution to support further reliable conclusions. That said, if you discover additional useful rules from a more detailed dataset, feel free to share them!
